Tech Titans of China: How China's Tech Sector is Challenging the World by Innovating Faster, Working Harder, and Going Global By: Rebecca Fannin

My thoughts on the book:

This book was in an entirely different category than the other two books that I have read so far this summer. In this book it focuses on how the Tech industry in China which has been dominated by Baidu, Alibaba, and Tencent is growing and the startup culture is exploding at rates faster than the US and other places globally. It was interesting to read through all of the different success stories that come with these startups in China. My only complaint of the book is that it felt very biased, as I guess you could argue that it has to be, towards Chinese technology and did not give United States companies the respect that they deserve for the effort they put in in innovating new technology and creating new advances in spaces like AI and machine learning.
Overall, this is a great book that highlights many of the things that I'm sure lots of people are wondering about, it was written in 2019, so it actually includes some talk about the brewing trade war that has been coming in the recent couple years under the Trump Presidency. I would be interested in seeing the next book in this series to see how this story between US - China plays out.

The Effortless Experience: Conquering the New Battleground for Customer Loyalty By: Matthew Dixon, Nick Toman, and Rick Delisi

My thoughts on the book:

This books includes many inside looks into how the customer experience landscape is changing through the years. From when it was solely about the product, and customer service was just something of an afterthought, it is important now to measure it in different ways than it was before. In this book, it lays out techniques like the Customer Effort Score, which focuses on scoring the transactional nature of the customer service, and realizing whether or not customers were provided with the service that is perceived to be of least effort. This is the future and the present of customer service, where self-service is king and the remaining phone calls are done for extremely complex issues that are still not trusted in self-service. I would have loved some talk about how to best implement and convince users towards giving self-service a shot. Additionally, I really appreciated how in the book, they provided many case studies of companies that took their advice and stood to profit on it in very specific measures. That allows them to gain credibility about the validity in their statements.
This book was the reading material for my summer internship at General Motors that will be starting later this summer, I am excited to see what things I can adapt from this book into General Motors, and how I can better the Customer Experience. I am looking forward to checking out the mass amount of data that there is and shaping it to give us more insight into how the customer service industry in GM can be modified and pushed towards the future!

Pour Your Heart Into It: How Starbucks Built a Company One Cup at a Time: Howard Schultz and Dori Jones Yang

My thoughts on the book:

This book was a very interesting look into the inner growth of one of the greatest companies of this generation or previous generation. It included insight into Howard Schultz as he grew up as a kid in the Projects, into the CEO of a multi-billion dollar business. Reading this now, more than 20 years after this book was published, a lot of the things he mentions still ring true in my mind, such as: the importance of putting your employees above all, and focusing on internal growth first before you move towards pleasing the customer. That is one belief that I have held for much of my life. Throughout this entire book, I loved to read about his little stories of how Starbucks was struggling and how he was able to overcome them through his efforts with his team. I thoroughly enjoyed reading about his work with the people that he brought in to help him run Starbucks. One of the biggest things that I took away from this book was when Howard Schultz mentioned that he only hires people who have done things before and people he can learn from. He also mentions how as an entrepreneur, that is one of the scariest things to do, because you begin to resign control over aspects of your company.
Throughout reading this book, I was able to get invaluable insight into how a company was built on common values, and how Howard was able to motivate so many people on his team to believe in his vision. I also felt very inspired hearing about his beginnings and how he was able to defy all odds and get to the place that he is at. My only complaint for the book was that at times, it felt like Howard was beginning to rant about how he was sad or angry that people viewed Starbucks in a certain way, without understanding why that came to be. It showed his humanity, which I appreciated but in a memoir I would have hoped to see some introspection into reasons behind those general feelings from the public.
